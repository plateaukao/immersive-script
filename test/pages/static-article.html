Early Beginnings

The first mechanical clocks appeared in Europe around the start of the fourteenth century. They were large, weight-driven machines installed in church towers, and they struck the hours for an entire town to hear.

These early devices had no faces or hands. Instead, they existed purely to ring bells, which is reflected in the word "clock" itself — it derives from the medieval Latin clocca, meaning bell. The Pendulum Revolution

In 1656, Christiaan Huygens built the first pendulum clock, improving accuracy from about fifteen minutes of drift per day to roughly fifteen seconds. It was one of the largest single leaps in the history of timekeeping.

The pendulum clock remained the world's most precise timekeeper for nearly three hundred years, until the invention of quartz oscillators in the twentieth century.

Key Properties of a Good Escapement

  • It must deliver impulses to the pendulum with minimal interference to its natural swing.
  • It must count oscillations reliably across changes in temperature and humidity.
  • It should be manufacturable with the tooling available to a small workshop.
